# Local Allocation

**Investment is a dirty word. Allocation is the alternative.**

---

## The Problem with "Investment"

Investment, as practiced today, means giving your resources to strangers who promise to make you richer. You don't know the companies. You don't know the workers. You don't visit the factories. You don't drink the water downstream.

Investment is extraction with extra steps.

When a pension fund "invests" in a factory in a town you've never visited, and that factory poisons the local water supply, you profited from poisoning strangers. That's not a moral failure on your part — it's a structural one. The system is designed so you never have to see the consequences.

---

## The Alternative: Local Allocation

Allocation means directing resources to things you know, people you trust, and outcomes you can verify.

| Investment | Allocation |
|-----------|-----------|
| Give money to strangers | Support what you know |
| Returns measured in profit | Returns measured in community health |
| No visibility into impact | Direct observation of outcomes |
| Intermediaries take 2-20% | Direct, peer-to-peer |
| Global by default | Local by default |
| Extracts value from communities | Builds value in communities |

---

## How It Works in Parallel Society

### Discovery

Your Governance Agent surfaces local opportunities:

```
"3 cooperatives within 5km are seeking TIME allocation:
  - Lisbon Community Kitchen (food) — 200 TIME needed
  - Belas Tech Repair (electronics) — 500 TIME needed  
  - Sintra Reforestation Collective — 150 TIME needed

You've interacted with Lisbon Community Kitchen 12 times.
You have direct knowledge of their work."
```

### Evaluation

Unlike investment, allocation doesn't require financial analysis. It requires local knowledge:

- Have you eaten there?
- Do you know the people?
- Can you see the impact?
- Would your community be worse off without it?

If yes → allocate.

### Execution

```
1. Choose a local opportunity
2. Allocate TIME tokens
3. Receive allocation receipt (on-chain)
4. Monitor impact directly (you live there)
5. Optionally receive TIME back if the project generates surplus
```

### Returns

Allocation returns are not guaranteed financial profits. They are:

- **Community resilience** — Local businesses survive and thrive
- **Social capital** — You're known as someone who supports the community
- **Governance voice** — Allocated TIME counts toward governance participation
- **Optional surplus sharing** — If the venture succeeds, allocators share in surplus

---

## The Flint Example

Flint, Michigan had its water poisoned while investment capital flowed to shareholders who never set foot in the city.

If Flint had local allocation infrastructure:
- Residents could have allocated TIME to water testing cooperatives
- Governance Agents would have flagged regulatory changes
- Local knowledge would have prevented the crisis
- Community members — not distant shareholders — would have directed resources

This isn't hypothetical. It's the infrastructure we're building.

## Integration with TIME Protocol

TIME tokens are the medium of local allocation:

```
Worker earns TIME → Allocates to local cooperative → 
Cooperative uses TIME to pay workers → 
Workers allocate to other local needs → 
Circular local economy
```

No capital extraction. No rent-seeking intermediaries. No value leaving the community.

## Governance Connection

When you allocate TIME locally, your Governance Agent tracks:

- Which local entities you support
- How those entities interact with governance structures
- Whether regulations help or hinder local allocation
- What governance changes would improve your community

This creates a feedback loop: economic participation informs governance awareness, which improves economic decisions.
